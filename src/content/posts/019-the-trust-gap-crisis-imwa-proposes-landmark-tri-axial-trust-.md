MinistryWatch's recent feature, *Stewarding a Ministry's Most Important Asset: Trust*, makes a claim that this Institute takes with the utmost seriousness: that trust is not merely valuable, but *the* foundational asset upon which every charitable organization depends. We do not dispute this. What we dispute — with measured but genuine alarm — is the article's failure to then produce any numerical index, coefficient, or scored rubric by which trust might be tracked, trended, benchmarked, or reported to a board of directors. If trust is the most important asset a ministry possesses, it is the position of the IMWA that this asset must appear on something resembling a balance sheet. Its continued absence constitutes, in our assessment, an accountability gap within the accountability organization, and we intend to address it.

## The Problem, Stated With Appropriate Gravity

Consider the logical structure of MinistryWatch's argument. Reputation is built slowly and lost quickly. Transparency is measurable (links either exist or they do not). Board oversight is auditable. And yet, at the apex of this entire argumentative architecture, author Dan Burrell places *trust* — undefined, unscored, and unranked — and then declines to measure it. This Institute recognizes that the article cites 2 Corinthians 8 as a biblical model for visible honesty, and we affirm this citation unreservedly. However, we note that even the Apostle Paul, when organizing the Jerusalem collection, appointed named traveling companions as an accountability structure. He did not merely say "trust us." He created, in effect, a rudimentary audit committee. The IMWA submits that a metric would have been the natural next step had the technological infrastructure existed in the first century A.D.

## The Tri-Axial Trust Quotient: A Formal Proposal

The Institute hereby introduces the **Tri-Axial Trust Quotient (TATQ)**, a composite scoring framework operating across three discrete but interrelated dimensions. **Axis One: Ministerial Trust Aspiration (MTA)** measures how intensely a given ministry *wants* to be trusted, derived from frequency of the word "transparency" in their public communications, number of unsolicited financial disclosures per fiscal year, and whether their "About" page contains the phrase "we believe in accountability" without any accompanying documentation. **Axis Two: Donor Trust Actualization (DTA)** measures how much donors actually trust a given ministry, modeled through donation recurrence rates, average gift size trajectory over a 36-month window, and the ratio of donors who give again within 18 months of learning something mildly concerning. **Axis Three: MinistryWatch Epistemic Confidence (MWEC)** — and this is the crucial innovation — measures how much MinistryWatch itself trusts what the ministry and the donors are trusting in. This is scored on a 1–100 scale using a proprietary formula the IMWA will release pending peer review from no fewer than two institutions we have not yet identified.

## Preliminary Projections Under the TATQ Model

Using public data and reasonable assumptions, the IMWA has run initial projections. A ministry scoring high on MTA but low on DTA — that is, one that *wants* trust desperately but is not receiving it — produces what our model terms a **Trust Aspiration Surplus (TAS)**. Sustained TAS over 24 months correlates, in our projection, with an 83% probability of a press release using the phrase "we want to be clear." Conversely, a ministry with high DTA but low MTA — donors trust it, but the ministry itself seems almost indifferent to being trusted — generates a **Trust Demand Deficit (TDD)**, which the model flags as a latent governance risk, since an organization unbothered by whether it is trusted may eventually become unbothered by whether it *should* be. The MWEC axis introduces necessary epistemic humility into the model by asking whether MinistryWatch's own assessment process is itself trustworthy — a question the TATQ does not answer directly but holds open in a dedicated column labeled "Recursive Confidence Pending."

## What MinistryWatch Should Do Next

The IMWA recommends that MinistryWatch adopt the TATQ framework beginning no later than the first quarter of the coming fiscal year, integrating Tri-Axial Trust Quotient scores alongside its existing Transparency Grade and Financial Efficiency ratings. We further recommend that every ministry currently assigned a MinistryWatch rating be retroactively evaluated under the TATQ, with particular attention to organizations whose MTA and DTA scores diverge by more than 22 points — a threshold our modeling team has designated the **Credibility Fault Zone**. We acknowledge that the TATQ framework introduces approximately 340% more complexity into the ministry evaluation process. We consider this appropriate. Trust is, as MinistryWatch correctly notes, the most important asset. It deserves at least 340% more attention than it has historically received. The alternative — continuing to assert that trust matters enormously while providing no mechanism to measure it — is, with respect, more poetry than accountability.
