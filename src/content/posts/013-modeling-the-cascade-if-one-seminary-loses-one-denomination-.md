MinistryWatch has reported that the United Methodist Church has removed Asbury Theological Seminary from its list of approved institutions for ordination candidates, citing the seminary's theological position on human sexuality. The Institute for Ministry Watch Accountability (IMWA) has reviewed this coverage and, following standard protocols, submitted the underlying data to our Denominational Severance Projection Unit for trend modeling. What emerged from that analysis is, to put it in the measured terms befitting this institution, a trajectory that warrants extended observation over several decades, or possibly the remainder of recorded Christian history.

## The Baseline Figures

At the time of the UMC action, Asbury Theological Seminary maintained approval relationships with a finite number of denominations. That number has now decreased by one. Our analysts at the IMWA have identified this as a unit rate of denominational severance that, if applied uniformly across the remaining approval relationships and extrapolated at a conservative linear projection, produces a terminus date for Asbury's institutional approval relationships sometime in the mid-to-late 21st century. However, if the rate is modeled exponentially — as severance events have a well-documented tendency to accelerate once institutional momentum is established — the projection window tightens considerably. The [IMWA Projection Table 7-C](/imwa/projection-table-7c), available upon written request to our offices — or, now, via the link just provided — illustrates this convergence with appropriate granularity.

## The Sector-Wide Implications

The IMWA does not stop at a single data point. That is not what we do. Our mandate requires us to ask: if one seminary loses one denomination's approval under conditions of theological disagreement, and if theological disagreement is a variable present across the broader landscape of American Protestant Christianity, what is the expected steady-state approval relationship count for the average accredited seminary by the year 2087? Our modeling, which assumes a constant theological disagreement coefficient of 0.73 (derived from our proprietary [Ecclesial Friction Index](/imwa/ecclesiastical-friction-index)), suggests the answer approaches, but does not technically reach, zero. We wish to be precise: we are not predicting the disappearance of seminaries. We are predicting the disappearance of their approval relationships, which is a meaningfully different outcome with only slightly less catastrophic institutional implications.

## A Note on the MinistryWatch Coverage Itself

MinistryWatch's reporting on this matter is competent, straightforward, and appropriately sourced. The IMWA commends this. However, we note that the article does not include a Denominational Severance Projection Table, nor does it model the compounding approval-loss scenarios that our Unit has now spent eleven days developing. This is not a criticism so much as an observation about the limits of conventional accountability journalism when confronted with data sets that demand extrapolation. MinistryWatch watches ministries. The IMWA watches MinistryWatch watching ministries. It is at this second level of remove that the truly consequential analytical work occurs.

## Recommendations and Forward Posture

The IMWA recommends that all remaining seminaries with denominational approval relationships conduct an immediate internal audit of those relationships, assigning each a Severance Probability Score using our forthcoming rubric, which will be released pending final review by our Committee on Rubrics. We further recommend that MinistryWatch establish a dedicated Approval Relationship Monitoring Desk to track the sector-wide trajectory our models have now clearly identified. Finally, we recommend that Asbury Theological Seminary be commended for providing the IMWA with what is, without question, the most productive extrapolation scenario our Projection Unit has encountered in the current fiscal year. The data has been generative. The models are running. The committee meets Thursday.
