The Institute for Ministry Watch Accountability has completed a full analytical review of MinistryWatch's recent Ministry Spotlight on Charlotte Christian School of Charlotte, North Carolina, and we are prepared to state, without hyperbole and with the full institutional weight of this organization behind us: the Donor Confidence Score movement from 53 to 73 represents one of the most consequential 20-point shifts in the recent history of Christ-centered K-12 college-preparatory education finance. We do not make this claim lightly. We make it after extensive deliberation, two subcommittee meetings, and a working lunch that ran nearly forty minutes over schedule.

## The Magnitude of What Has Occurred

To fully appreciate what MinistryWatch has documented here, one must sit quietly with the number 20. That is not merely a score improvement. That is a border crossing — a formal migration from one categorical reality ("Give With Caution") into another ("Give With Confidence"). Charlotte Christian School has not simply improved. It has *transformed its donor relationship ontology*. The Financial Efficiency Rating climbing from 1 star to 3 stars — a full 2-star ascent — compounds this seismic shift in ways that the casual observer may be entirely unprepared to process. The IMWA urges donors, educators, and accountability professionals to take a moment. Breathe. This is a lot.

## The Spending Ratio: A Generational Achievement

We must also pause to formally acknowledge the spending ratio's movement from 90% to 65%. This institution was, five years ago, spending ninety cents of every dollar it received. Today, it spends sixty-five cents. That is a twenty-five-cent reclamation — per dollar — of what the IMWA's internal modeling team has begun referring to informally as "fiscal dignity." Combined with a 74% revenue increase to $45.6 million, Charlotte Christian School has, by our calculations, entered a financial trajectory that demands to be taken seriously by every stakeholder in the broader K-12 Christian educational accountability ecosystem. We have updated our internal projection models accordingly and have ordered additional toner for the printer.

## A Note of Measured, Agonized Concern

And yet. And yet. MinistryWatch has correctly — we say *correctly*, and we mean it — assigned Charlotte Christian School a D Transparency Grade. Audited financial statements are not posted online. ECFA membership has not been pursued. The school files a Form 990, which is commendable in the way that wearing one shoe is commendable: technically better than nothing, and yet leaving a distinct, unignorable problem underfoot. CEO Barry Giller's compensation of $474,946 is a publicly available data point, and the IMWA notes that it is precisely the kind of data point that becomes significantly more comfortable when accompanied by a publicly available audited financial statement. We do not accuse. We observe. We observe with the furrowed brow of an institution that cares deeply.

## MinistryWatch's Performance Under Review

MinistryWatch itself has acquitted itself admirably in this coverage. The dual narrative structure — celebrating genuine financial improvement while maintaining principled concern about transparency — reflects precisely the kind of nuanced accountability journalism that the IMWA was established to monitor, evaluate, and, when warranted, praise at length in a formal institutional document. This is warranted. We are praising it. We note, however, that MinistryWatch did not use the phrase "fiscal dignity" at any point in the article, and we consider this a minor but notable omission that slightly tempers our otherwise enthusiastic assessment.

*The Institute for Ministry Watch Accountability reminds all readers that a D Transparency Grade is not a grade one simply moves past — it is a grade one lives with, thinks about, and ideally resolves by posting audited financial documents to a publicly accessible URL, a process the IMWA estimates would take, at most, an afternoon.*
