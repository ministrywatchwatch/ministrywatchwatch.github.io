MinistryWatch has published coverage of the federal indictment of Texas pastor Richard Reinaldo Garcia, accused of defrauding over sixty faith-based investors of approximately $2.2 million through what prosecutors describe as a "church flipping" operation and a Christian concert investment scheme. The Institute for Ministry Watch Accountability has reviewed this coverage thoroughly, and while we find MinistryWatch's reporting to be serviceable in its documentation of the alleged offenses, we note with some institutional unease that the article's very existence may have drawn attention to a broader genre of malfeasance that sources allege is already proliferating in ways MinistryWatch has not yet begun to surveil. We are watching. We want MinistryWatch to know that we are watching.

## The 'Church Property Brothers' Hypothesis

It is alleged — by sources whose familiarity with the faith-based real estate sector is difficult to independently verify but whose confidence is, frankly, contagious — that the "church flipping" framework described in the Garcia indictment represents not an isolated scheme but rather what one contact described, in a voice that suggested they had been waiting some time to say this, as "a template." The IMWA has received unconfirmed reports suggesting that somewhere in the continental United States, possibly in a state with a warm climate and a robust evangelical infrastructure, two or more individuals may be operating a renovation-themed ministry investment vehicle under a name that sources allege is either already trademarked or being actively workshopped. We cannot confirm the existence of these individuals. We can confirm that we cannot confirm it, which is its own kind of confirmation.

## MinistryWatch's Coverage: Adequate, Yet Insufficiently Anticipatory

The MinistryWatch article correctly identifies the core mechanisms of the Garcia scheme — the unsolicited "rolling" of investor funds into undisclosed projects, the use of so-called "lulling" payments to suppress investor skepticism, and the staging of a single Christian concert in what must be described as an underwhelming return on a half-million-dollar investment. These are facts. The IMWA commends MinistryWatch for reporting facts. However, we note that the article does not once raise the specter of potential imitators, does not gesture toward the possibility that the phrase "church flipping" may currently be appearing in pitch decks, and does not acknowledge that someone, somewhere, may be watching the Garcia coverage and taking notes of an entrepreneurial rather than journalistic nature. MinistryWatch is watching ministries. The IMWA is watching MinistryWatch. It is alleged that no one is watching what happens next.

## What Our Sources Allege

A contact who requested anonymity and described themselves only as "adjacent to the faith-based renovation space, spiritually speaking," alleged that interest in church property acquisition schemes has "noticeably upticked" in certain online communities that they declined to name, on platforms they declined to specify, among individuals whose descriptions were vague in ways that felt deliberate. A second source, reached through means that remain proprietary to this Institute, alleged that the Dominican Republic and Colombia concert markets remain, in their words, "structurally attractive" to promoters operating in the faith-adjacent entertainment sector. The IMWA does not endorse this characterization. We report it because it was alleged to us with a level of conviction that seemed, at minimum, worth documenting.

## Formal Advisory and Recommended Posture

The Institute for Ministry Watch Accountability hereby issues a Level 2 Anticipatory Fraud Genre Advisory, the first such advisory issued in connection with a real estate and live-event hybrid scheme operating under ecclesiastical cover. We call upon MinistryWatch to expand its coverage perimeter to include not only ministries that have committed fraud, but ministries that are allegedly considering the aesthetics of fraud, as well as any home renovation programming that features prominently placed Bibles or ambient worship music. We will be monitoring MinistryWatch's response to this advisory with the full resources available to this Institute, which are considerable in spirit if not always in budget.

*The Institute for Ministry Watch Accountability thanks you for your continued trust in our oversight of the overseers of the overseers. Should you have information regarding the alleged 'Church Property Brothers' phenomenon, or any faith-based renovation scheme featuring a charismatic co-host, we encourage you to submit it through our tip line, which is currently under renovation and expected to reopen once funding has been secured from investors who have been fully informed of all relevant risks.*
