The Institute for Ministry Watch Accountability does not convene its Emergency Numerical Oversight Subcommittee lightly. The subcommittee has, in fact, met formally only twice in its history: once in response to a rounding discrepancy in a 2019 fundraising transparency index, and once due to what turned out to be a scheduling conflict. We convene it now a third time. MinistryWatch has published, without apparent hesitation or evident remorse, a list of ministries whose Donor Confidence Scores fall below the threshold of 50 — a number that is, we remind our readers, exactly half of 100. The implications of this midpoint demarcation are not trivial. They are, in the considered judgment of this Institute, seismic.

## The Score Itself: What Is It, and Why Does It Haunt Us

The Donor Confidence Score, as deployed by MinistryWatch, purports to represent a composite assessment of a ministry's financial transparency, accountability practices, and organizational governance. That much is publicly stated. What is *not* publicly stated — and what this Institute has now devoted considerable institutional energy to investigating — is the precise algorithmic weighting applied to each component of this score. A score, we must emphasize, that is capable of falling below 50. We do not yet know if geopolitical variables have been introduced into this weighting. We do not yet know if ecumenical relationships — or, more pointedly, *anti-ecumenical* postures — have been encoded into the composite. We do not yet know. But we are watching. We wish to be absolutely clear on this point: **we are watching.**

## The Geopolitical and Anti-Ecumenical Question Cannot Be Dismissed

It would be convenient — indeed, it would be comfortable — to assume that a Donor Confidence Score is simply a score about donor confidence. The Institute is not in the business of comfort. We are in the business of accountability, specifically the accountability of those who perform accountability, and we take that recursive responsibility with the gravity it deserves. The question before us is straightforward: has the MinistryWatch scoring methodology been calibrated, whether consciously or through systemic institutional drift, to disadvantage ministries whose theological commitments place them outside the ecumenical mainstream? Has the algorithm, in short, been laundered of its apparent neutrality? These are not accusations. They are, in the formal language of our discipline, *watchfulness postures.* We adopt them without apology.

## A Preliminary Taxonomy of Concern

This Institute identifies three distinct tiers of concern arising from the present publication. The first tier is **numerical**: the choice of 50 as a threshold is not self-evidently correct, and no public methodology document has been produced to justify it over, say, 47, or 53, or a rolling three-year average expressed as a percentile band. The second tier is **categorical**: the instruction to "withhold giving" from listed ministries represents a directive of considerable donor-behavioral consequence, and the Institute questions whether MinistryWatch has fully modeled the downstream effects of such language on the philanthropic ecosystem of global evangelical Christianity. The third tier is **observational**: we are watching. We have already said this, but it bears repetition and will continue to bear repetition until such time as it no longer needs to be said, which is not now.

## What This Institute Will Do Next

The Emergency Numerical Oversight Subcommittee has been formally tasked with three deliverables. First, a full review of all publicly available MinistryWatch scoring documentation, to be completed upon its availability, which at this time is ongoing. Second, a formal written inquiry to MinistryWatch requesting clarification on weighting variables, methodological controls, and any geopolitical or ecumenical adjustment factors, whether applied directly or by inference. Third, a watching. The Institute will, in the interim, continue to monitor MinistryWatch's deployment of the Donor Confidence Score with the full complement of our analytical resources, which are considerable, and our institutional resolve, which is absolute. We do not prejudge the outcome of this review. We simply note that a score below 50 has been assigned to certain ministries, that someone decided 50 was the line, and that we find both of these facts, taken together, to be a great deal to sit with.

*The Institute for Ministry Watch Accountability thanks you for your continued vigilance. Vigilance, as always, is its own reward. Dr. Heft-Morrow's watchfulness posture remains elevated.*
