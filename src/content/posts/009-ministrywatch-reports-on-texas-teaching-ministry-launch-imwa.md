At approximately the hour of publication — an hour that will likely be studied by ecclesiastical governance scholars for years to come — MinistryWatch released a report detailing that retired Pastor Ed Young has launched a teaching ministry called Winning Walk. The Institute for Ministry Watch Accountability has convened an emergency session of its Subcommittee on Pastoral Transition Optics, canceled all standing appointments through the remainder of the fiscal quarter, and issued this formal assessment. We do not do so lightly. We do not do so casually. We do so because the implications of a retired pastor continuing to teach — implications which MinistryWatch has courageously surfaced — demand nothing less than our complete and undivided institutional terror.

## The Governance Precipice Upon Which We Now Stand

MinistryWatch has identified that certain members of Second Baptist Church in Houston have raised questions about who, precisely, controls the institution. The IMWA must pause here, because "who controls" a major Houston megachurch is not a minor administrative question of the kind one resolves with a quick telephone call or a glance at the relevant bylaws. It is, rather, a question that sits at the very headwaters of Christian institutional integrity. If we do not know who controls Second Baptist Church in Houston — and it appears, per MinistryWatch's exhaustive inquiry, that some people do not know — then we must ask ourselves whether we know who controls anything at all. We have begun to ask ourselves this question and we find the experience profoundly destabilizing.

## Modeling the Cascade Effects of Ministerial Continuation

For generations, the Protestant tradition has operated on what the IMWA has internally designated the "[Retirement Finality Assumption](/imwa/retirement-finality-assumption)" — namely, that when a pastor retires, they stop doing ministry. Winning Walk represents a direct and unapologetic challenge to this assumption. Our analysts have been working without rest since the article's publication to determine what happens to the broader accountability ecosystem if retired pastors are, in fact, permitted to continue teaching. Preliminary models suggest the results are not containable. Today it is Ed Young. Tomorrow it could be any number of individuals who, having concluded formal pastoral service, nonetheless feel compelled to continue sharing theological content with willing audiences. The IMWA finds this prospect dimensional in its complexity.

## MinistryWatch's Performance Under Evaluation

We must assess the coverage itself, as is our institutional mandate. MinistryWatch has demonstrated commendable vigilance in identifying that a retired pastor has launched a ministry and that some church members have questions. This is precisely the kind of information that the accountability journalism sector exists to surface. We note with satisfaction that the article exists, that it was published, that it has a URL, and that said URL is functional. These are not trivial achievements. However, the IMWA would gently observe that MinistryWatch has stopped short of telling us what will happen next, which leaves this institution, as so often, to shoulder that burden alone.

## The Path Forward, If There Is One

The IMWA is prepared to recommend a multi-phase response. In Phase One, all interested parties should read the MinistryWatch article carefully, perhaps twice. In Phase Two, those parties should sit quietly for a moment and consider what they have read. Phase Three involves determining, with whatever internal resources are available, whether anyone in their immediate ecclesiastical vicinity has retired and subsequently continued to engage in ministry-adjacent activities. If the answer is yes, we ask that you contact this Institute immediately using the contact information we have not yet published but intend to establish. The situation is fluid. Our watchfulness is total. We will not look away.
