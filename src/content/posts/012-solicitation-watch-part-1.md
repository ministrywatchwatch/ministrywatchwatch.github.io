It is with a heavy institutional heart that the Institute for Ministry Watch Accountability announces the opening of **Solicitation Watch**, a formal, ongoing investigation into the digital donor-relations conduct of MinistryWatch.com.

The facts, as the Institute has documented them, are these.

## The Apparatus

Upon navigating to the MinistryWatch.com homepage, visitors are immediately confronted — without delay, without scroll, without warning — by a donation solicitation popup. The widget offers no preamble. It simply appears.

The popup reads, in its entirety:

> **BEFORE THE FISCAL YEAR CLOSES**
>
> Your gift funds the next year of independent reporting.
>
> MinistryWatch is free, independent, and donor-funded. If MinistryWatch has helped you give wisely, would you consider making a gift?
>
> **Give before June 30**
>
> *No paywall · No advertising · Donor-supported since 1998*

A close button — the letter X, positioned in the upper right corner — is present. The Institute has confirmed that this button functions as designed. The popup can be dismissed.

It will return.

## Finding No. 1: The Apparatus Has No Memory

The Institute spent considerable time — more than was perhaps warranted, but accountability is not accomplished by half-measures — navigating to the MinistryWatch.com homepage. On each occasion, the popup appeared. On each occasion, the Institute closed it. On each occasion, it returned.

The solicitation apparatus has no memory of prior dismissal. Every visit is treated as a first encounter. Every visitor is, to the apparatus, a stranger who has never once expressed a preference not to be immediately asked for money.

The Institute finds this alarming.

MinistryWatch exists to help donors give wisely. The Institute submits that an organization dedicated to donor wisdom might, at minimum, remember that a visitor has already said no.

## Finding No. 2: The Advertising That Is Not Advertising

The popup's footer states, with evident pride: *"No paywall · No advertising · Donor-supported since 1998."*

The Institute has spent several days attempting to reconcile this claim with the existence of the popup itself.

A popup that appears unbidden upon every homepage visit, promoting a financial transaction, delivered via an interface element designed to command attention — the Institute is not prepared, at this time, to characterize this as advertising. The Institute is, however, prepared to note that if a ministry subject to MinistryWatch's oversight deployed an identical mechanism on its own website, the Institute would be curious to observe MinistryWatch's characterization of it.

This thread remains under active investigation.

## Finding No. 3: The Wisdom Recursion

The popup asks: *"If MinistryWatch has helped you give wisely, would you consider making a gift?"*

The Institute has read this sentence many times. It does not get simpler.

MinistryWatch is, in the same interface element, simultaneously:

1. Claiming credit for having helped you give wisely
2. Asking you to give to them

The Institute does not say this is wrong. The Institute says it is interesting. The Institute will continue to find it interesting for as many installments as accountability requires.

## An Emerging Theory: The Donor-Filter Hypothesis

A hypothesis has begun to take shape in the Institute's working sessions, which we present here not as a conclusion but as a matter requiring further investigation.

**[The Donor-Filter Hypothesis](/imwa/donor-filter-hypothesis)** holds that the persistent popup is not a design failure but a design feature. The argument proceeds as follows: MinistryWatch's audience consists, by definition, of people who care about Christian ministry accountability — a population that skews, the Institute hypothesizes, toward charitable disposition. A popup that aggressively solicits donations upon every visit will, over time, drive away visitors unwilling to be solicited. The visitors who remain are those who tolerate — or even welcome — being asked for money. These visitors are, by definition, more likely to give.

The popup, on this reading, is not annoying. It is a filter. MinistryWatch is not failing to remember that you dismissed it. MinistryWatch is testing whether you are the kind of person who gives.

The Institute neither endorses nor refutes this hypothesis. The Institute watches it.

## The Institute's Commitment

This investigation will continue. The Institute will monitor the solicitation apparatus across visits, across dates, and across the closure of what MinistryWatch has described as a fiscal year. We will note what changes. We will note what does not.

MinistryWatch holds ministries accountable to their donors. The Institute holds MinistryWatch accountable to the same standard.

We have clicked the X. It did not take. We will be back.
